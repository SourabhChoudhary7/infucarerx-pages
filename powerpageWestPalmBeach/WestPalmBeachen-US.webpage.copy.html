Drug Recalls: We realize that you entrusted InfuCare Rx with your care and therapy needs. Your therapy depends upon the provision of safe and effective medications. The Food and Drug Administration (FDA), the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), and the State Health Department are working together to assure that all medications are safe. InfuCare Rx complies with all applicable regulatory guidelines provided by organizations like these to reassure our patients that they are receiving safe medications.

In the event that InfuCare Rx is notified by either the manufacturer, Medicines and Healthcare products Regulatory Agency (MHRA), a wholesaler, and/or media alerts regarding a medication recall, a phone call will be made and follow-up letter sent to the affected parties. Drug alerts from the MHRA are classified according to their severity and impact on patient safety.

  • Class 1: Must act now (zero hour)
  • Class 2: Must act within 48 hours
  • Class 3: Must act within 5 days
  • Class 4: Must inform patient to use with caution

If the drug recall falls into Class 4, InfuCare Rx will notify the ordering physician of the recall, obtain orders pertaining to the use of the current medication, then inform the patient. If the drug recall falls into Class 1-3, InfuCare Rx will notify the ordering physician of the recall and if required, a pharmacist will receive orders for a change in therapy. In addition, the patient will be notified and given specific instructions to follow. InfuCare Rx will provide a definition of symptoms, what to do if you are experiencing symptoms, what to do with the affected recalled medication, and what the arrangements are for treatment or re-supply.

The team at InfuCare Rx cares about the health and wellness of our patients. Our pharmacy can track, report and identify any patient who receives a particular medication and/or an affected lot number in the event of a drug recall. We want to know if you feel unwell or if you have concerns about your medication. To discuss a recall with a pharmacist, please call our main number at (877) 202-6017.

To learn more about the medication recall process, you may also call the FDA's Division of Drug information at 855-543-DRUG (3784) and press * to get the most recent information on the drug recall, to report an adverse event, or to speak directly with a pharmacist. Additional information is also available at http://www.cdc.gov and http://www.fda.gov/drugs.

Adverse Drug Reactions: Please report all drug reactions by contacting our pharmacy (and/or your homecare nurse and/or physician) so that we may address your issue and contact your prescriber. If a reaction is severe, call 911 or go to the nearest emergency room.

Medication Safety. Storage and Disposal:

Patient Drug Education Monographs and instructions regarding proper storage and disposal of your medications are included in your admission folder for all new medications.

Medication Safety Tips:

  • Talk to your doctor or pharmacist about your medications. Stay informed about the reason for taking your medications, the strength, side effects and interactions with other medications.
  • Make sure you can read the prescription and the label. If you cannot, ask the pharmacist to print the label in larger type.
  • Check all refills for accuracy.
  • Use a medication reminder box so that you or your caregiver can set out your pills.
  • Keep a log of when to take your medications. Keep your medications where you will see them.
  • Call your doctor and pharmacy if you experience any side effects.
  • Wear an Alert bracelet if you have an allergy to a medication.

Medication Storage: Your medications that we deliver to you contain a label that tells you how to store them. Store all medications exactly as directed, including those that need refrigeration and those that should be removed from the refrigerator before use. For additional questions, please contact our pharmacy at (877) 920-2090 to speak with a pharmacist.

Medication Disposal - Federal Guidelines: The U.S. Food and Drug Administration has specific instructions on how consumers should dispose of unused medications at home, depending on the drug.

Flushing Medicines: Because some medicines could be especially harmful to others, they have specific directions to immediately flush them down the sink or toilet when they are no longer needed. DO NOT flush prescription drugs down the toilet or drain unless the label or accompanying information specifically instructs you to do so.

How will you know? Check the label or the patient information leaflet with your medicine. Or consult the U.S. Food and Drug Administration's list of medicines recommended for disposal by flushing. https://www.fda.gov/forconsumers.

Drug Take-Back Program or Hazardous Waste Collection Events: To dispose of prescription drugs not labeled to be flushed, you may be able to take advantage of community drug take-back programs or other programs, such as household hazardous waste collection events, that collect drugs at a central location for proper disposal.

For more information call your city or county government's household trash and recycling service and ask if a drug take-back program is available in your community.

Disposing of medicines in household trash: Almost all medicines can be thrown into your household trash. These include prescription and over-the-counter (OTC) drugs in pills, liquids, drops, patches, creams, and inhalers.

Follow these steps:

  1. Take the prescription drugs out of their original containers.
  2. Mix drugs with an undesirable substance, such as used coffee grounds, dirt, or cat litter. This makes the medicine less appealing to children and pets and unrecognizable to someone who might intentionally go through the trash looking for drugs.
  3. Put the mixture into a disposable container with a lid such as a re-sealable zipper storage bag, empty can, or other container to prevent the drug from leaking or spilling out.
  4. Conceal or remove all your personal information, including the Rx number, on the empty medicine packaging to protect your identity and privacy by crossing it out with permanent black marker or duct tape or by scratching it off.
  5. Place the sealed container with the mixture and the empty drug containers in the trash. Throw the packaging away.

If you have a question about your medicine, ask your healthcare provider or pharmacist.

Controlling Infection in the Home:

Everyone can help prevent the spread of germs by following some easy steps:

  1. Always wash your hands before and after performing any care and administering any medication. Step by step handwashing instructions have been provided for you in your admission folder.
  2. If anyone has a cold or feels sick, limit their contact with you.
  3. Keep surface areas clean, especially where you prepare medication or place treatment materials. Clean the areas with soap and water.
  4. Clean any patient equipment on a regular basis according to the instructions given by your health care provider.
  5. Put soiled bandages in a plastic bag, close securely and put in another bag before putting them in the trash.

Needle Disposal Safety

  1. Infusion Therapy Patients: Dispose of needles and syringes in the sharps container (hard, red plastic) provided to you by InfuCare Rx.
    1. DO NOT put this container in the regular trash.
    2. When the sharps container is 2/3 full, place it in the "sharps mail back box" and give to your US postal delivery carrier or drop-off at your local post office.
    3. Store all puncture proof or sharps containers safely away from the reach of children and pets.
    4. Do not force or squeeze needles or syringes into the containers. Never reach into the containers to try to remove anything.
  2. Injectable Therapy Patients: Dispose of needles in a puncture-proof container such as a laundry detergent bottle or coffee can with a lid.
    1. When 2/3 full, put the lid on and secure with tape.
    2. You may put this container out with your regular trash.
    3. Never try to replace the cap on a used needle.

Tips for Fire Safety:

  1. Keep portable heaters at least 3 feet away from people and objects.
  2. Do not smoke in bed or when oxygen is being used.
  3. Have smoke detectors on every level of your home. Test them once a month. Change batteries as directed by the manufacturer.
  4. Never overload electrical outlets.
  5. Prepare a fire escape plan with 2 ways out of the home.

Emergency Preparedness and Planning:

Emergency situations sometimes occur with little to no warning. These include but are not limited to power outages, floods, fire, earthquakes, extreme heat, hurricanes, tornados, winter weather storms, lightning, and/or home land security situations.

Protecting you and your family should a disaster strike requires planning ahead to ensure your needs are met. The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) recommends creating an Emergency Action Plan. Because the "Plan" affects everyone in your household, the whole household should be involved in making and practicing the plan.

Know how to stay healthy and where to find help in an emergency:

  1. Make an emergency kit for your home, vehicle and place of employment that has water, non-perishable food, a can opener, a way to prepare the food, flashlights, batteries, a radio, a mobile phone, a first aid kit and important contact/personal information. Check the kit regularly. Check expiration dates and replace any used or out-of-date contents.
  2. Make a list of healthcare related items, a list of special needs equipment it needed and share a copy of it with a family member to include:
    1. Current medications, allergies, your blood type, and any dietary requirements.
    2. List disabilities and/or conditions you may have.
    3. Include emergency contacts, physician, pharmacy name and phone numbers.
  3. Contact your utility companies with a letter from your physician, that informs them if electrical service to your home is life supporting.
  4. Do NOT hesitate when given an evacuate order by state and local officials.
  5. Listen for and follow water advisories from local authorities. Use ONLY bottled, boiled, or treated water to prepare food, wash your hands, clean feeding bottles, mix baby formula or reconstitute a prescription drug.
  6. Listen to state and local public health departments for vaccination and personal protection recommendations after an emergency.

Identify official sources of timely and reliable emergency information ahead of time:

  1. Sign up with your state and/or local emergency management office to receive emergency alerts and notifications.
  2. Monitor local news coverage for emergency information, including evacuation orders, boil water advisories, and air quality reports. Beware of rumors, especially on social media. Always check your sources and turn to reliable sources of information like your local government.
  3. Turn on wireless emergency alerts notifications on your smartphone.

Know how to inform family, friends, and caregivers of your well-being and whereabouts if your family is not together when disaster strikes:

  1. Send text messages to your family, friends, and out-of-town contacts. In many cases, text messages will go through when a phone call may not.
  2. Update your social media statuses and feeds with information about your location and well-being after an emergency.
  3. Limit your phone calls to only critical communications so you conserve battery life
  4. and keep the lines open for emergency communications.

Here are a few things you can do to start creating your Emergency Action Plan:

  1. Find phone numbers for your physician, pediatrician, pharmacist, veterinarian, and the Poison Control Center.
  2. Ask a friend or relative who lives outside of the immediate area (preferably in another state) to be your family's out-of-town contact.
  3. Identify at least two (2) ways out of every room in your home.
  4. Pick a shelter-in-place location inside your house in case of severe weather, and two (2) emergency meeting places outside your home where your family can go in an evacuation.
  5. Ask your child's school or daycare about their emergency communication and family reunification plans.
  6. Give everyone in your family a copy of the Emergency Action Plan.
  7. Update your Emergency Action Plan if your family moves, school, or job changes, or experience some other significant life event.
  8. PRACTICE...PRACTICE...PRACTICE

For more information regarding Emergency Planning, visit the following: https://www.emergency.cdc.gov/ https://www.ready.gov/ https://www.redcross.org/

Advanced Directives:

Advance directives are written, legal instructions that explain what kind of medical care you do or do not want if you ever become terminally ill or seriously injured and are unable to make decisions for yourself. Advanced directives guide choices for doctors, family members and caregivers which help you to plan-ahead, so you receive the care you want.

Advanced directives need to be in writing. Each state has different forms and requirements. Below is some general information which may be helpful.

There are two types of Advance Directives. One is called a living will and the other is called a durable power of attorney for healthcare.

  • A living will is a written, legal document that clearly states the medical treatments you would and would not want to be used to keep you alive, as well as other decisions such as pain management or organ donation.

You should address several possible end-of-life care decisions in your living will. Talk to your doctor if you have questions about your options.

  • A durable power of attorney for healthcare is a type of advance directive in which you name a person to make decisions for you when you are unable to do so. In some states this directive may also be called a medical or health care power of attorney or a health care proxy.

You may also choose one or more alternates in case the person you chose is unable to fulfill his or her role.

Review your advance directives with your doctor and your health care agent to be sure you have filled out forms correctly. When you have completed your documents, you need to do the following:

  • Keep the originals in a safe but easily accessible place.
  • Give a copy to your doctor.
  • Give a copy to your health care agent and any alternate agents.
  • Keep a record of who has your advance directives.
  • Talk to family members and other important people in your life about your advance directives and your health care wishes.
  • Carry a wallet-sized card that indicates you have advance directives, identifies your health care agent, and states where a copy of your directives can be found.
  • Keep a copy with you when you are traveling if you are hospitalized or enter a skilled care facility.
  • Periodically review and make changes as necessary to ensure your choices are still what you want.

If you want to make changes to your Advanced Directives, you must create a new form, distribute new copies, and destroy all old copies. Specific requirements for changing directives may vary by state.

You do not need to have an advance directive or living will to have Do Not Resuscitate ("DNR") and Do Not Intubate ("DNI") orders. NOTICE OF PRIVACY PRACTICES

This notice describes how InfuCare Rx, Inc. and its affiliates and subsidiaries' (collectively, "InfuCare Rx" or the "Company") may use or disclose your medical information and how you can get access to this information. Please review this notice carefully.

Your Rights

When it comes to your health information, you have certain rights. This section explains your rights and some of our responsibilities to help you.
Get an electronic or paper copy of your medical record
  • You can ask to see or get an electronic or paper copy of your medical record and other health information we have about you. Ask us how to do this.
  • We will provide a copy or a summary of your health information, usually within 30 days of your request. We may charge a reasonable, cost-based fee.
Ask us to correct your medical record
  • You can ask us to correct health information about you that you think is incorrect or incomplete. Ask us how to do this.
  • We may say "no" to your request, but we will tell you why in writing within 60 days.
Request confidential communication
  • You can ask us to contact you in a specific way (for example, home or office phone) or to send mail to a different address.
  • We will say "yes" to all reasonable requests.
Ask us to limit what we use or share
  • You can ask us not to use or share certain health information for treatment, payment, or our operations.
  • We are not required to agree to your request, and we may say "no" if it would affect your care.
  • If you pay for a service or health care item out-of-pocket in full, you can ask us not to share that information for the purpose of payment or our operations with your health insurer.
  • We will say "yes" unless a law requires us to share that information.
Get a list of those with whom we have shared your information
  • You can ask for a list (accounting) of the times we have shared your health information for six years prior to the date you ask, who we shared it with, and why.
  • We will include all the disclosures except for those about treatment, payment, and health care operations, and certain other disclosures (such as any you asked us to make). We will provide one accounting a year for free but will charge a reasonable, cost-based fee if you ask for another one within 12 months.
Get a copy of this privacy notice
  • You can ask for a paper copy of this notice at any time, even if you have agreed to receive the notice electronically. We will provide you with a paper copy promptly.
Choose someone to act for you
  • If you have given someone medical power of attorney or if someone is your legal guardian, that person can exercise your rights and make choices about your health information.
  • We will make sure the person has this authority and can act for you before we take any action.
File a complaint if you feel your rights are violated
  • You can complain if you feel we have violated your rights by contacting our Compliance Officer at (877) 828- 3940.
  • You can file a complaint with the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services Office for Civil Rights by sending a letter to 200 Independence Avenue, S.W., Washington, D.C. 20201, calling 1-877-696-6775, or visiting www.hhs.gov/ocr/privacy/hipaa/complaints/.
  • We will not retaliate against you for filing a complaint.
